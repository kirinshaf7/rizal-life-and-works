Rizal's Life: Higher Education and Life Abroad

Exploring how Rizal's international education shaped his ideas and advocacy

University Education in Manila

After graduating with highest honors from the Ateneo Municipal de Manila in 1877, Rizal pursued higher education in Manila, marking the beginning of his journey as a scholar and intellectual.

The University of Santo Tomas in the late 19th century

University of Santo Tomas (1877-1882)

Rizal enrolled at the University of Santo Tomas (UST), the oldest university in Asia, where he pursued multiple courses:

Philosophy and Letters

Rizal initially enrolled in this program in 1877, following his brother Paciano's advice to "study all you can." He completed his Bachelor of Arts degree with excellent grades.

Medicine

In 1878, Rizal enrolled in the Faculty of Medicine at UST. He was particularly interested in ophthalmology, partly motivated by his mother's developing eye problems. While at UST, he won prizes in literary contests and continued to develop his artistic skills.

Land Surveying

Simultaneously with his medical studies, Rizal also took courses in land surveying at the Ateneo Municipal. He completed this program and received his license as a land surveyor in 1881, though he never practiced this profession.

Experiences at UST

Rizal's time at the University of Santo Tomas was marked by both academic achievement and growing disillusionment:

  • Academic Excellence:

    Despite his multiple courses of study, Rizal maintained excellent academic performance, winning literary competitions and impressing his professors.

  • Discrimination:

    Rizal experienced discrimination as a Filipino student. Spanish professors and Dominican friars who ran the university often favored Spanish students over native Filipinos.

  • Outdated Teaching Methods:

    Rizal became increasingly frustrated with the university's outdated teaching methods, which emphasized memorization over critical thinking and practical application.

  • Growing Nationalism:

    During this period, Rizal's nationalist sentiments grew stronger as he witnessed the disparities in treatment between Spanish and Filipino students.

"I am going to Europe to study. In the Philippines, there is no hope of progress for me. The authorities see in me nothing but a suspect individual who may someday cause trouble." — José Rizal, in a letter explaining his decision to leave for Europe

Education in Europe

Dissatisfied with the education system in the Philippines and seeking broader intellectual horizons, Rizal decided to continue his studies in Europe. This decision would profoundly shape his intellectual development and political consciousness.

José Rizal during his time in Europe

Journey to Spain (1882)

In May 1882, Rizal secretly left the Philippines for Spain, without informing Spanish authorities who might have prevented his departure. This journey marked the beginning of a decade of travel, study, and intellectual growth across Europe.

Universidad Central de Madrid (1882-1885)

In Madrid, Rizal continued his medical education and pursued additional studies:

Medical Studies

Rizal enrolled in the Universidad Central de Madrid to continue his medical education. He completed his licentiate in medicine in 1884 and received his medical degree in 1885.

Philosophy and Letters

Simultaneously, Rizal also pursued a degree in Philosophy and Letters, which he completed in 1885 with excellent grades.

Languages and Other Studies

Always eager to expand his knowledge, Rizal took additional courses in languages, art, and other subjects. He became fluent in French, German, and English during this period, adding to his already impressive linguistic abilities.

Cultural Immersion

Rizal immersed himself in Spanish culture, visiting museums, attending theaters, and participating in intellectual discussions in cafés and cultural centers.

Specialized Medical Training

After completing his medical degree in Madrid, Rizal pursued specialized training in ophthalmology:

1885-1886

Paris

Rizal worked as an assistant to Dr. Louis de Wecker, a renowned ophthalmologist in Paris, where he refined his skills in eye surgery.

1886

Heidelberg

Rizal studied under Dr. Otto Becker at the University of Heidelberg in Germany, further specializing in ophthalmology.

1887

Berlin

In Berlin, Rizal worked with Dr. Karl Ernst Schweigger, a distinguished German ophthalmologist, and attended lectures by the famous pathologist Rudolf Virchow.

Intellectual and Political Development

Rizal's years in Europe were not only a time of academic study but also a period of significant intellectual and political growth:

The Propaganda Movement

In Spain, Rizal became actively involved in the Propaganda Movement, a group of Filipino students and intellectuals advocating for reforms in the Philippines. He contributed articles to La Solidaridad, the movement's newspaper, calling for equal treatment of Filipinos, representation in the Spanish Cortes, and other reforms.

Literary Works

During his time in Europe, Rizal wrote his two famous novels that exposed the abuses of Spanish colonial rule: "Noli Me Tángere" (Touch Me Not), published in Berlin in 1887, and "El Filibusterismo" (The Subversive), published in Ghent in 1891. These novels are considered his greatest contributions to Philippine literature and nationalism.

Scientific Research

Rizal conducted scientific research in various fields, including anthropology, ethnology, and natural sciences. In Berlin, he was admitted to the Berlin Ethnological Society and the Berlin Anthropological Society in recognition of his scientific work.

Historical Research

Rizal spent time in the British Museum in London researching pre-colonial Philippine history. He annotated Antonio de Morga's "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas" (Events in the Philippine Islands), an important historical account from 1609, to highlight the advanced civilization of pre-colonial Philippines.

First edition of "Noli Me Tángere," published in Berlin in 1887

Life in Europe

Beyond his academic and political activities, Rizal's life in Europe was rich and varied:

  • Cultural Experiences:

    Rizal immersed himself in European culture, visiting museums, attending operas and plays, and studying European art and architecture. These experiences broadened his worldview and influenced his own artistic work.

  • Social Life:

    Despite his busy schedule of study and writing, Rizal maintained an active social life. He formed friendships with fellow students, intellectuals, and artists from various countries.

  • Personal Relationships:

    During his time in Europe, Rizal had several romantic relationships, most notably with Josephine Bracken, whom he met later in Dapitan, and Leonor Rivera, his childhood sweetheart who eventually married someone else when communication between them was cut off.

  • Financial Struggles:

    Despite coming from a relatively wealthy family, Rizal often faced financial difficulties during his time in Europe. His family's resources were strained by legal battles with Spanish authorities, and Rizal sometimes had to live frugally, taking on translation work and other jobs to support himself.

Return to the Philippines and Exile

After years abroad, Rizal returned to the Philippines in 1887, hoping to help his family and practice medicine. However, his return was short-lived:

1887

First Return

Rizal returned to the Philippines after the publication of "Noli Me Tángere," which had angered Spanish authorities and friars. He practiced medicine briefly in Calamba.

1888

Return to Europe

Facing threats and surveillance, Rizal left the Philippines again for Europe, where he continued his advocacy and completed "El Filibusterismo."

1892

Second Return and La Liga Filipina

Rizal returned to Manila and founded La Liga Filipina, a civic organization promoting reform. Days later, he was arrested and exiled to Dapitan in Mindanao.

1892-1896

Exile in Dapitan

During his four years of exile, Rizal practiced medicine, taught school children, engaged in scientific research, and undertook community development projects.

Rizal during his exile in Dapitan

Legacy of Rizal's Education

Rizal's extensive education and experiences abroad profoundly shaped his ideas and contributions to Philippine society:

Medical Practice

Rizal used his medical training to serve communities, particularly during his exile in Dapitan where he treated hundreds of patients, including performing successful eye surgeries.

Educational Reform

Having experienced both Philippine and European educational systems, Rizal advocated for educational reforms that emphasized critical thinking, practical skills, and scientific knowledge.

Scientific Contributions

Rizal's scientific research, particularly in zoology, botany, ethnography, and anthropology, contributed to the scientific understanding of the Philippines and its people.

Political Thought

His exposure to liberal and progressive ideas in Europe informed his political thought, which emphasized the importance of education, civic engagement, and peaceful reform as paths to national development.

"I desire the happiness of my country. I shall gladly give my life for it, and even more gladly give my life to save many innocent persons." — José Rizal, in his final letter before execution
