Rizal's Life: Family, Childhood, and Early Education

Exploring the formative years that shaped the Philippine national hero

The Mercado-Rizal Family

José Rizal was born into a prosperous family that belonged to the principalia (native elite) class in the Philippines. Understanding his family background provides important context for his later life and works.

The Mercado-Rizal family tree

Parents

Francisco Engracio Mercado (1818-1898)

Rizal's father was a prosperous farmer who leased land from the Dominican religious order. He was educated in Latin and Spanish, which was unusual for native Filipinos at that time. Francisco sent all his children to good schools, reflecting his belief in the value of education.

Teodora Alonso Realonda (1827-1911)

Rizal's mother was one of the most highly educated women in Calamba at that time. She was well-versed in literature and mathematics and was fluent in Spanish. Teodora played a crucial role in Rizal's early education and instilled in him a love for learning.

Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso, Rizal's parents

Siblings

José Rizal was the seventh of eleven children. His siblings played important roles in his life and supported his education and later political activities:

Saturnina Rizal (1850-1913)

The eldest of the Rizal siblings, Saturnina was a supportive sister who helped finance Rizal's education in Europe. She married Manuel Hidalgo, who was later exiled to Bohol for alleged involvement in anti-Spanish activities.

Paciano Rizal (1851-1930)

Paciano was Rizal's only brother and one of his greatest influences. He was educated at the Colegio de San José in Manila and was involved in liberal and nationalist movements. Paciano financially supported Rizal's studies in Europe and served as his political mentor.

Narcisa Rizal (1852-1939)

Narcisa was known for her intelligence and business acumen. After Rizal's execution, she was the one who discovered his unmarked grave at Paco Cemetery and arranged for a small marble plaque to mark the site.

Olympia Rizal (1855–1887)

A close sibling and correspondent of José. She died at a young age due to childbirth.

Lucia Rizal (1857–1919)

Married Mariano Herbosa, who died without being given a Catholic burial due to his relation to Rizal, which stirred controversy and resentment.

Maria Rizal (1859–1945)

Known for her practicality and strong will. She often visited José in exile and remained loyal to family values.

Concepción Rizal (1862–1865)

Died at the age of 3. Her death deeply affected young José Rizal, who remembered her throughout his life.

Josefa Rizal (1865–1945)

A member of the Katipunan (code name "Panggoy"). She remained unmarried and suffered from epilepsy.

Trinidad Rizal (1868–1951)

Also a member of the Katipunan. She was the one who received José’s farewell poem “Mi Último Adiós,” which he hid inside an alcohol lamp.

Soledad Rizal (1870–1929)

The youngest sibling. She became a teacher and married Pantaleon Quintero. José called her his favorite little sister.

Childhood in Calamba

José Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, a prosperous town on the shores of Laguna de Bay. His childhood experiences in Calamba profoundly influenced his worldview and later writings.

Reconstruction of Rizal's childhood home in Calamba, Laguna

Early Influences

  • Family Environment:

    Rizal grew up in a comfortable home with a private library, which was rare for Filipino families at that time. The family valued education, culture, and intellectual pursuits.

  • Natural Surroundings:

    The natural beauty of Calamba and Laguna de Bay fostered Rizal's appreciation for nature, which is evident in his later writings and sketches.

  • Social Awareness:

    Even as a child, Rizal witnessed the social inequalities and abuses of the Spanish colonial system, including the harassment his mother experienced when she was falsely accused of attempting to poison her cousin and imprisoned for nearly two years.

  • Religious Upbringing:

    Rizal was raised in a devoutly Catholic household, which shaped his moral values while also making him aware of the abuses committed by some members of the clergy.

"I remember the first book that made me cry. It was the story of the moth and the flame. My good mother was seated by the window, and she was relating to us the story of the moth that, having seen a flame, was attracted to it and burned itself in it." — José Rizal, recalling his childhood

Early Education

Rizal's education began at home and continued through various schools, laying the foundation for his intellectual development:

1865-1867

Home Education

Rizal's mother, Teodora, taught him to read and write at the age of three. She also instilled in him a love for literature, telling him stories and poems.

1867-1869

Private Tutors

Rizal received private tutoring from Maestro Celestino and Maestro Lucas Padua, who taught him Latin and Spanish.

1869-1871

Biñan

At the age of eight, Rizal was sent to Biñan, Laguna, to study under Maestro Justiniano Aquino Cruz, where he excelled in Latin and Spanish.

1872-1877

Ateneo Municipal

Rizal enrolled at the Ateneo Municipal de Manila, a school run by the Jesuits, where he excelled academically and received excellent grades in all subjects.

Academic Excellence

From an early age, Rizal demonstrated exceptional intellectual abilities:

  • Languages:

    By the time he was a teenager, Rizal was already proficient in Spanish, Latin, and Tagalog. He would eventually learn over 20 languages throughout his life.

  • Literature and Poetry:

    Rizal began writing poetry at a young age, including his first poem "Sa Aking Mga Kabata" (To My Fellow Children), reportedly written when he was just eight years old.

  • Art:

    He showed talent in drawing and sculpture from an early age, skills he would continue to develop throughout his life.

  • Science and Mathematics:

    Rizal excelled in science and mathematics at the Ateneo, laying the foundation for his later studies in medicine.

The Ateneo Municipal de Manila where Rizal studied from 1872 to 1877

Early Character Formation

Several key experiences and traits shaped Rizal's character during his early years:

Physical Challenges

Rizal was small in stature and physically frail as a child. To compensate, he took up physical exercise, including fencing, wrestling, and martial arts, developing discipline and determination.

Love for Learning

From an early age, Rizal demonstrated an insatiable curiosity and love for learning. He was an avid reader who spent hours in his family's library.

Sense of Justice

Witnessing injustices, including his mother's wrongful imprisonment, instilled in Rizal a strong sense of justice and a desire to fight against oppression.

Religious Faith

Rizal's deep religious faith, nurtured by his family and his education at the Ateneo, provided a moral foundation for his life while also making him critical of religious abuses.

Early Signs of Nationalism

Even in his youth, Rizal showed signs of the nationalist sentiments that would define his later life:

  • The GOMBURZA Execution:

    The execution of three Filipino priests (Gomez, Burgos, and Zamora) in 1872 had a profound impact on the 11-year-old Rizal, awakening his awareness of the injustices of Spanish colonial rule.

  • Family Experiences:

    The harassment experienced by his family, including his mother's imprisonment and the constant threat of eviction from their lands, made Rizal acutely aware of the abuses of the colonial system.

  • Early Writings:

    His early poems and essays already showed a deep love for his native language and culture, as well as a critical view of Spanish colonialism.

Did You Know?

  • Rizal's full name was José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda.
  • The family adopted the surname "Rizal" (originally a second name) to avoid association with the Mercado family, who were being watched by Spanish authorities.
  • Rizal was small in stature, standing only about 5 feet 3 inches tall.
  • As a child, Rizal kept a diary where he recorded his observations about nature and society.
  • Rizal's first teacher was his mother, who taught him to read and write before he was five years old.
